# 03 — Footage & Legal (Compliant-First)

> This is the document that keeps the channel alive. F1's rights-holder
> (Formula One Management / FOM, under Liberty Media) is among the most active
> copyright enforcers on YouTube. Channels using race footage get **Content ID
> claims, manual takedowns, and copyright strikes** routinely — even on
> genuinely transformative commentary. Three strikes deletes the channel. Plan
> as if you will never get to use raw race footage, and treat anything you *can*
> use as a bonus.
>
> **Not legal advice.** This is a practical risk-management strategy. For
> anything money-significant, get a media/IP lawyer in your jurisdiction.

## The mindset

You don't need race clips to make great F1 video. The strongest F1 analysis
channels lean on **original graphics and data visualisation**, not borrowed
footage. Compliant-first isn't a compromise — for an analysis/insight brand it's
often a *better, more distinctive* look.

## Footage sourcing tiers (green → red)

### ✅ Green — safe, build the channel on these
- **Original motion graphics & data viz** — standings, gap charts, tyre
  strategy, telemetry-style overlays, track maps, timelines, tables. *Your IP.*
  This should be the visual backbone, especially for Analysis/Explainers.
- **Properly licensed stock** — paid stock libraries (clips + stills) with a
  licence that covers monetised YouTube. Read the licence terms.
- **Creative Commons** assets, used within their licence (attribution, etc.).
- **Your own footage** — anything you shoot (e.g. at a circuit, if you go),
  diecast/model b-roll, screen-recordings of *your own* graphics.
- **Public-domain** historical material (verify status carefully — "old" ≠
  "public domain").
- **Official embeds** — embedding an official F1/team YouTube video or social
  post *via the platform's embed feature* keeps the view/rights with the owner
  (use sparingly and in a genuinely supplementary way, not as your core video).
- **AI-generated / stock imagery** for illustrative/abstract shots (cars,
  paddock mood, abstract speed) — no rights entanglement.

### 🟠 Amber — possible but get it right
- **Press/wire photos** — only with the correct licence (Getty etc.). Don't pull
  images off Google.
- **Team/sponsor assets** — sometimes share-able for editorial/fan use, but
  check each owner's terms; don't assume.
- **Brief stills under commentary** — lower risk than moving footage, but still
  claimable. Minimise and transform (annotate, analyse).

### 🔴 Red — avoid (this is the whole point of "compliant-first")
- **Race broadcast / onboard / highlight clips** from FOM or broadcasters.
  Even short, even with commentary, these draw Content ID claims and strikes.
  Your "Short clips under fair use" option was the alternative you *didn't*
  pick — and FOM's enforcement is exactly why.
- **Ripping other creators' videos** (their footage, their graphics, their
  edits, their voiceover).
- **Re-uploading another channel's clips** even with reaction over the top.

## "Fair use / fair dealing" — why we don't rely on it

- It's a **legal defence, not a permission** — you only find out if it holds
  *after* you've been claimed/sued.
- **Content ID doesn't care about fair use** — it's automated and will claim/
  demonetise/block regardless; disputes are slow and risky.
- UK "fair dealing" (you're UK-based per the account) is **narrower** than US
  fair use — limited categories (criticism/review, news reporting, quotation,
  parody) with strict conditions. Don't build a business model on it.

## Transcript scraping — the research-vs-reproduction line

You want to "scan other YouTube videos and extract text scripts." Here's how to
do that safely:

- ✅ **OK as research signal:** pulling transcripts to understand *what topics
  are covered, which angles dominate, and which questions go unanswered*. You're
  studying the market, then writing your own original take. This is analogous to
  reading competitors before writing your own piece.
- ⚠️ **Grey / avoid at scale:** automated mass-scraping can breach **YouTube's
  Terms of Service** regardless of copyright. Prefer the official **YouTube Data
  API** for metadata and captions where possible; keep volumes reasonable;
  don't redistribute the raw transcripts.
- 🔴 **Not OK:** reproducing or lightly-rewording another creator's script.
  That's copyright infringement *and* it makes your channel derivative and
  worthless. The LLM step must **synthesise across many sources into an original
  thesis**, never paraphrase one.

**Rule of thumb:** transcripts go *in* as research; only *your own words and
analysis* come *out*.

## Defamation (matters for the Drama pillar)

Drama/storytelling about real people (drivers, principals) carries libel risk,
especially under **UK defamation law** (claimant-friendly).
- Stick to **reported, sourced facts**; attribute ("according to…").
- Clearly separate **opinion** ("in my view…") from stated fact.
- Avoid asserting unproven wrongdoing, cheating, or motives as fact.
- Be especially careful with anything affecting someone's professional
  reputation.

## YouTube policy hygiene

- **Monetisation / "inauthentic content":** YouTube has tightened rules on
  mass-produced/low-effort AI content. *Semi-automated with a human angle,
  original graphics, and genuine analysis is the safe side of that line* — pure
  auto-generated reaction slop is not. Your chosen setup is deliberately on the
  right side; keep it there.
- **Disclosure:** label synthetic/altered media where YouTube requires it.
- **Three-strike rule:** copyright strikes (not Content ID claims) are the
  channel-killer. Compliant-first is strike-avoidance by design.

## Pre-publish checklist (every video)

- [ ] Every visual is Green-tier, or Amber with licence/terms confirmed.
- [ ] No FOM/broadcaster race footage. No ripped creator content.
- [ ] Music is royalty-free/licensed for monetised use.
- [ ] Facts verified against a primary source; quotes attributed.
- [ ] Drama pieces: opinion vs fact clearly separated, no unproven claims.
- [ ] Required AI/altered-media disclosures set.
- [ ] Transcripts used only as research — script is original.
